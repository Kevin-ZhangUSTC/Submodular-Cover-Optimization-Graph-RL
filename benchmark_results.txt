============================================================================
  Experiment 2: Beating Greedy on Non-Submodular Instances
  (instances where greedy is provably suboptimal vs brute-force)
============================================================================

  BACKGROUND: The posterior trace f(S) = tr(Σ_S) is NOT submodular.
  Marginal gains can INCREASE as more sensors are selected (supermodular
  behaviour), so greedy — which picks the sensor with highest current
  marginal gain — can be trapped in locally-good but globally-suboptimal
  orderings.  A GNN+RL policy trained with HIGH EXPLORATION (no greedy
  imitation) is free to discover these globally-better sensor sets.

  Non-submodularity verification (random N=8 PD matrices):
  Submodularity violations: 98/3000 triples (3.3%)  →  objective is NOT submodular

  Adversarial instance A (N=8):
    Brute-force OPTIMAL : 5 sensors   (trace=1.2767 ≤ ε=1.3470)
    Greedy              : 6 sensors   (trace=0.9715 ≤ ε=1.3470)
    Gap (greedy−optimal): 1 sensor(s)  ← greedy is SUBOPTIMAL

  Adversarial instance B (N=7, 7 valid 4-sensor solutions):
    Brute-force OPTIMAL : 4 sensors   (trace=1.7341 ≤ ε=1.7461)
    Greedy              : 5 sensors   (trace=1.0665 ≤ ε=1.7461)
    Gap (greedy−optimal): 1 sensor(s)  ← greedy is SUBOPTIMAL

  Training GNN+RL on instance B (NO imitation, entropy=0.15, 3000 eps, 10 seeds)...
    GNN+RL best         : 4 sensors  (satisfied=True)

  THREE-WAY COMPARISON (instance B):
    Optimal : 4 sensors
    GNN+RL  : 4 sensors    ✓ BEATS GREEDY
    Greedy  : 5 sensors

  Greedy suboptimality rate (500 random feasible N=8 instances):
    Greedy strictly suboptimal: 30/483 feasible instances (6.2%)
    Max gap observed          : 1 sensor(s)
  → On ~6% of instances, a smarter algorithm
    CAN use fewer sensors than greedy.
